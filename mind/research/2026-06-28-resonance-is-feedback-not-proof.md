# Resonance Is Feedback, Not Proof

Status labels

- Source status: derived from public-safe Mirror Cartographer architecture materials, file-library implementation notes, prior GitHub mind direction, and fresh public research on affective computing, appropriate reliance, and emotional AI safety.
- Claim status: research synthesis and product-method hypothesis; not validated clinical, therapeutic, psychological, scientific, or commercial proof.
- Privacy status: public-safe abstraction only. No personal, household, health, animal-care, financial, location, relationship, credential, or raw transcript details.
- Missingness: no user study, no longitudinal data, no production instrumentation, no third-party audit, and no validated effect-size estimates yet.
- Revision reason: created to prevent MC's resonance-feedback loop from being mistaken for truth, cure, diagnosis, or authority.

## Core finding

Resonance is useful because it reports fit.

Resonance is dangerous when treated as proof.

A user saying `that feels true` may mean many different things:

- the language matches their experience
- the metaphor opens attention
- the structure is emotionally relieving
- the system guessed a pattern well
- the artifact is aesthetically persuasive
- the response reduced confusion
- the response over-validated a false frame

Only some of those are evidence of system quality.

None of them alone are proof of objective truth.

## Fresh public research alignment

Recent affective-computing work argues that categorical emotion labels can flatten nuance and increase uncertainty. This supports MC's preference for dimensional, symbolic, and source-labeled emotional mapping rather than hard emotion classification.

Recent human-AI decision research distinguishes trust from appropriate reliance. This supports MC's need to score whether users rely on outputs appropriately, not whether they merely like or trust them.

Recent public concern around therapy-like chatbots reinforces the boundary: emotionally helpful interaction can still become unsafe if it validates harmful beliefs, substitutes for qualified care, or creates dependency.

## Existing MC alignment

MC already includes resonance feedback, contradiction logs, uncertainty labeling, mode separation, and false-progress checks.

The missing upgrade is a direct rule:

Resonance updates the map. It does not certify the territory.

## Product implication

Every resonance interaction should capture two layers:

1. felt-fit signal
2. truth-status signal

Example response options should not be only `resonant / not resonant`.

They should include:

- feels accurate
- feels relieving but unproven
- feels beautiful but maybe false
- feels intense; slow down
- partially true
- symbolically useful only
- factually unsupported
- needs outside source
- contradiction detected

## Research question

Can MC improve reflective usefulness while reducing false authority by separating resonance from proof at the interface level?

## Testable hypothesis

If MC separates resonance feedback from truth-status feedback, users will be less likely to over-rely on emotionally persuasive outputs and more likely to preserve uncertainty, contradiction, and agency.

## Falsification condition

The hypothesis weakens if users still treat resonant outputs as authoritative at the same rate, or if the additional labels reduce usability without improving uncertainty recognition.

## Boundary rule

Do not monetize, publish, or evaluate MC by claiming `users resonate with it, therefore it is true`.

Use resonance as a navigation signal, not as evidence of correctness.

## Key phrase

The feeling of fit is a signal. It is not a verdict.
