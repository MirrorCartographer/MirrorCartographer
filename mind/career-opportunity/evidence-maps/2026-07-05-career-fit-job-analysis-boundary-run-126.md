# Evidence Map — Career Fit Requires Job-Analysis Evidence

Date: 2026-07-05
Run: Evidence Engine 126
Area: AI opportunity work / career strategy
Status: claim narrowed; evidence boundary added

## Claim tested

"Mirror Cartographer / AI opportunity work can identify the user's best-fit career from personal pattern analysis, symbolic strengths, and AI-assisted reasoning."

## Updated claim status

Previous implied claim:

- A strong internal model of the user can identify the right career direction.

Updated bounded claim:

- A strong internal model can generate career hypotheses, but a career-fit claim is not evidence-based until it is mapped to explicit job requirements, observable work samples, constraints, labor-market conditions, and feedback from real selection or performance signals.

Confidence: moderate for the boundary; low for any specific career recommendation until tested.

## Evidence found

### Source 1 — EEOC: employment tests and selection procedures

Source: U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, "Employment Tests and Selection Procedures"  
URL: https://www.eeoc.gov/laws/guidance/employment-tests-and-selection-procedures

Relevant facts:

- Employers use many selection procedures, including cognitive tests, personality tests, medical exams, credit checks, criminal background checks, work samples, simulations, performance tests, and realistic job previews.
- The EEOC says tests and selection procedures can be effective for identifying qualified applicants, but can violate federal anti-discrimination law if used discriminatorily or if they disproportionately exclude protected groups without legal justification.
- For disparate-impact analysis, a selection procedure must be job-related and consistent with business necessity.
- The challenged practice should evaluate skills as related to the particular job, not merely general skill.
- EEOC best practices say selection procedures should be validated for the position and purpose, remain updated as job requirements change, and not be adopted casually without understanding limits and scoring.

Evidence implication:

- A career-fit claim must be job-specific. General statements such as "good at systems thinking" or "strong symbolic reasoning" are not enough. The claim needs role requirements, scoring criteria, and evidence that the person's demonstrated outputs match the role.

### Source 2 — O*NET: occupational data model

Source: O*NET Resource Center, "O*NET Data Collection Overview"  
URL: https://www.onetcenter.org/dataCollection.html

Relevant facts:

- O*NET describes itself as a comprehensive database of worker attributes and job characteristics.
- O*NET uses multi-method data collection, including analyst ratings, job incumbents, occupational experts, employer job postings, government programs, machine learning, NLP, and web research.
- O*NET spans about 900 occupation profiles and over 55,000 jobs, with portions refreshed annually and job titles/software skills updated more frequently.

Evidence implication:

- Career hypotheses should be mapped to an occupational model, not only personal narrative. O*NET provides a useful baseline for tasks, skills, knowledge, abilities, work contexts, and labor-market signals.

### Source 3 — O*NET: content model

Source: O*NET Resource Center, "The O*NET Content Model"  
URL: https://www.onetcenter.org/content.html

Relevant facts:

- O*NET organizes job and worker information into worker characteristics, worker requirements, experience requirements, occupational requirements, occupation-specific information, and workforce characteristics.
- The model includes abilities, work styles, skills, knowledge, education, experience/training, licensing, work activities, work context, tasks, job titles, labor-market information, and occupational outlook.
- O*NET defines several abilities relevant to the user's AI opportunity work, including fluency of ideas, originality, problem sensitivity, deductive reasoning, inductive reasoning, information ordering, category flexibility, speed of closure, flexibility of closure, and visualization.

Evidence implication:

- A credible "best-fit career" artifact should translate personal strengths into O*NET-like variables and then test them against actual occupations and job postings.

## Fact / inference separation

### Facts

- Employers commonly use structured selection procedures, including work samples and simulations.
- Selection procedures need job-relatedness, validity, and awareness of adverse impact when used for employment decisions.
- O*NET provides structured occupational variables for tasks, skills, abilities, work context, and labor-market information.
- O*NET data are maintained through multiple sources and refreshed over time.

### Inferences

- Mirror Cartographer career reasoning should treat "career fit" as a hypothesis, not a conclusion.
- Personal-symbolic modeling may help generate unusual career hypotheses, but it does not validate employability or role fit by itself.
- A strong portfolio is most useful when it is explicitly mapped to job tasks and selection criteria.
- The user's best next career direction should be evaluated through work-sample evidence and external feedback, not only introspective alignment.

## Evaluation criterion added

### MC-CAREER-FIT-VALIDATION-01

A career recommendation must include all six evidence layers before being labeled strong:

1. Role definition
   - Specific occupation, job family, or job posting named.
   - Core tasks listed.

2. Requirement mapping
   - Skills, abilities, knowledge, work context, and constraints mapped using O*NET-like categories.

3. User evidence mapping
   - Each claimed user strength linked to an observable artifact, past role, work sample, or documented outcome.

4. Work-sample test
   - At least one task simulation or portfolio artifact scored against role-relevant criteria.

5. External signal
   - Recruiter response, hiring-manager feedback, peer review, interview outcome, paid work, or measurable audience/customer behavior.

6. Falsification condition
   - Clear conditions that would lower confidence, such as repeated no-response applications for well-matched roles, failed task simulations, poor external review, or evidence that role constraints conflict with user constraints.

Strength labels:

- Speculative: personal pattern only.
- Plausible: pattern plus role mapping.
- Supported: role mapping plus work sample.
- Strong: work sample plus external feedback.
- Verified: repeated external outcomes or paid performance in the role.

## Test plan

### MC-CAREER-FIT-PILOT-01

Goal:

- Convert career recommendations from narrative confidence into tested hypotheses.

Protocol:

1. Select 10 target roles from AI, research, trust/safety, product, knowledge architecture, accessibility, and evaluation work.
2. For each role, extract tasks, skills, abilities, work context, communication load, meeting load, credential requirements, salary band, remote availability, and portfolio expectations.
3. Map the user's evidence to each role:
   - Navy nuclear systems experience.
   - Food Lion operations/management.
   - Rover/pet-care responsibility.
   - Mirror Cartographer architecture artifacts.
   - AI evaluation / evidence-engine artifacts.
   - Nervous-system and accessibility design work.
4. Score each role from 0–3 on:
   - Task alignment.
   - Evidence strength.
   - Constraint fit.
   - Income potential.
   - Remote/minimal-meeting feasibility.
   - Portfolio demonstrability.
   - Hiring accessibility without degree.
5. Build one role-specific work sample for the top three roles.
6. Submit or show the work samples to at least five external reviewers or real applications.
7. Update confidence only after external signals arrive.

## Falsification checklist

The claim "this career is a strong fit" should be downgraded if any of the following occur:

- The recommendation cannot be mapped to specific tasks.
- Claimed strengths cannot be linked to observable evidence.
- The role requires credentials or communication patterns that conflict with user constraints.
- A work sample cannot be produced at competitive quality.
- External reviewers do not understand the artifact's relevance to the role.
- Applications with role-specific materials repeatedly produce no response.
- The work is emotionally appealing but economically weak.
- The job market has low remote availability or high meeting dependency.
- The role's real tasks differ from the symbolic interpretation of the role.

## Next proof needed

MC-CAREER-FIT-PILOT-01 should be run on 10 real job postings, not abstract job titles. The next proof is a scored comparison table showing which role has the strongest evidence after job-analysis mapping and at least one work-sample test.

## Bottom line

Mirror Cartographer can be useful for career hypothesis generation because it preserves unusual strengths and constraints that standard resumes flatten. But career fit is not proven by identity coherence, visual polish, or internal confidence. It becomes stronger evidence only when mapped to job requirements, tested through work samples, and checked against external hiring or performance signals.
