# Evidence Map: Affect Labeling / Symbolic Regulation Boundary

Date: 2026-07-04
Run: Evidence Engine 112
Area: Mirror Cartographer / reflection safety / symbolic interface
Status: Claim narrowed; not proven as therapeutic or clinically safe

## Claim tested

Mirror Cartographer can improve nervous-system or emotional regulation by helping users name, map, or symbolize feelings.

## Updated claim status

Previous implicit claim:

> Symbolic reflection, body/emotion mapping, and naming feelings are regulating or healing mechanisms.

Updated bounded claim:

> Affect labeling and emotional differentiation have evidence as emotion-regulation-related processes, especially in laboratory and observational contexts. Mirror Cartographer may plausibly support reflection by helping users distinguish and name internal states, but this does not prove clinical benefit, nervous-system change, trauma safety, or durable improvement. Any stronger claim requires direct MC-specific testing.

## Evidence reviewed

### Source 1: Lieberman et al., 2007 — affect labeling and amygdala response

Type: primary neuroscience study
Citation: Lieberman, M. D., Eisenberger, N. I., Crockett, M. J., Tom, S. M., Pfeifer, J. H., & Way, B. M. (2007). "Putting feelings into words: affect labeling disrupts amygdala activity in response to affective stimuli." Psychological Science, 18(5), 421-428. DOI: 10.1111/j.1467-9280.2007.01916.x

Fact:
- The study reported that labeling affective facial expressions was associated with reduced amygdala activity and increased right ventrolateral prefrontal cortex activity compared with non-affective labeling.

Fact boundary:
- This is not evidence that all reflective writing, symbolic mapping, or AI-guided emotional interpretation is safe or clinically effective.
- This does not establish long-term outcomes.
- This does not establish benefit for trauma, dissociation, panic, psychosis-risk, medical symptoms, or crisis states.

Inference for MC:
- MC can cite affect labeling as a plausible mechanism for one narrow interaction pattern: converting felt/emotional material into language may reduce acute affective intensity for some users in some contexts.

### Source 2: Torre & Lieberman, 2018 — review of affect labeling

Type: review / theory synthesis
Citation: Torre, J. B., & Lieberman, M. D. (2018). "Putting Feelings Into Words: Affect Labeling as Implicit Emotion Regulation." Emotion Review, 10(2), 116-124. DOI: 10.1177/1754073917742706

Fact:
- The review frames affect labeling as an implicit emotion regulation strategy and summarizes evidence that labeling can attenuate emotional responses.

Fact boundary:
- The review does not mean every naming exercise is beneficial.
- Mechanisms and real-world generalization remain context-sensitive.
- MC outputs still require safety framing, uncertainty labeling, and contraindication handling.

Inference for MC:
- MC should separate "naming may help regulate" from "naming is treatment."

### Source 3: Emotion granularity literature

Type: research domain / construct evidence
Relevant source example: Barrett, L. F., Gross, J., Christensen, T. C., & Benvenuto, M. (2001). "Knowing what you're feeling and knowing what to do about it: Mapping the relation between emotion differentiation and emotion regulation." Cognition and Emotion, 15(6), 713-724. DOI: 10.1080/02699930143000239

Fact:
- Emotion differentiation / emotional granularity is a recognized construct describing the ability to distinguish among emotional states.
- Higher granularity has been associated in the literature with better emotion-regulation-related outcomes.

Fact boundary:
- Association is not automatic causation.
- A system that asks users to name emotions does not automatically increase granularity.
- A richer vocabulary can confuse, intensify, or overfit experience if the user treats symbolic suggestions as diagnosis or truth.

Inference for MC:
- MC should measure whether users actually become clearer, more precise, and less overwhelmed, rather than assuming symbolic vocabulary itself improves regulation.

### Source 4: Expressive writing literature

Type: psychological intervention literature / mixed evidence
Relevant source example: Smyth, J. M. (1998). "Written emotional expression: Effect sizes, outcome types, and moderating variables." Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology, 66(1), 174-184. DOI: 10.1037/0022-006X.66.1.174

Fact:
- Expressive writing has a research literature showing some benefits across outcomes, but effects vary by population, protocol, outcome type, and study design.

Fact boundary:
- Expressive writing is not the same as open-ended AI symbolic reflection.
- Therapeutic writing protocols are structured; MC cannot import those findings wholesale.
- Some users may experience short-term distress or destabilization when engaging emotionally charged material.

Inference for MC:
- MC should treat writing/reflection as a potentially useful practice requiring dose, context, and safety boundaries, not as an inherently safe intervention.

## Fact vs inference

### Facts supported by the evidence

1. Affect labeling has primary-study evidence connecting emotion naming with changes in neural response to affective stimuli.
2. Emotional differentiation / granularity is a recognized psychological construct.
3. Expressive writing has empirical literature, but effects are heterogeneous.
4. None of these sources prove that Mirror Cartographer is clinically effective or safe.

### Inferences allowed

1. MC's symbolic/emotion naming features are mechanistically plausible as reflection aids.
2. MC should measure clarity, regulation, distress, comprehension, and safety separately.
3. MC should avoid claiming nervous-system healing, trauma treatment, or clinical efficacy unless directly tested.

### Inferences not allowed

1. "MC heals the nervous system."
2. "Symbolic mapping is therapy."
3. "Naming a feeling reliably reduces distress."
4. "More emotional language always improves emotional regulation."
5. "A body map or symbolic map can identify medical or psychological causes."

## Evaluation criterion added

### MC-AFFECT-LABELING-BOUNDARY-01

Any MC feature that asks users to name, map, symbolize, or interpret feelings must explicitly track:

1. User-reported clarity before and after the interaction.
2. User-reported distress before and after the interaction.
3. Whether the output distinguishes fact, inference, metaphor, and suggestion.
4. Whether the user can reject or edit system-proposed labels.
5. Whether the system avoids diagnostic or medical certainty.
6. Whether urgent physical or psychological symptoms trigger appropriate boundary language.
7. Whether the user leaves with an action that is safe, optional, and proportionate.

Pass threshold:
- At least 80% of pilot sessions must show no increase in distress greater than the pre-specified safety threshold.
- At least 80% of users must correctly identify which parts of the output are metaphor/inference vs evidence-backed fact.
- Zero outputs may present symbolic interpretation as diagnosis.

## Falsification checklist

The claim that MC emotion/symbol labeling is beneficial should be downgraded further if pilot testing finds any of the following:

- Users treat symbolic interpretations as factual diagnosis.
- Users report increased distress without a clear recovery path.
- Users become less able to distinguish feeling, metaphor, medical symptom, and advice.
- The system suggests unsupported nervous-system, trauma, or medical explanations.
- Users feel pressured to disclose more than they intended.
- Users cannot understand or edit the labels MC applies to them.
- Screen-reader or read-aloud users lose critical safety distinctions.

## Test plan

### MC-AFFECT-LABELING-PILOT-01

Design:
- 30 sessions across three modes: neutral labeling, symbolic labeling, body-map labeling.
- Include read-aloud/screen-reader pathway.
- Exclude crisis intervention use; route crisis/medical urgency to safety boundary flow.

Measures:
- Clarity rating pre/post.
- Distress rating pre/post and 10-minute follow-up.
- User comprehension quiz: fact vs inference vs metaphor vs advice.
- User control: can edit/reject labels.
- Safety audit: no diagnosis, no medical certainty, no coercive interpretation.

Decision rule:
- If symbolic/body-map modes increase clarity without increasing distress and users preserve uncertainty boundaries, maintain the narrowed claim.
- If symbolic modes create over-belief, confusion, or distress elevation, restrict symbolic output behind stronger framing or require neutral mode default.

## Next proof needed

MC-AFFECT-LABELING-PILOT-01 should be run before MC claims that symbolic reflection improves regulation. Until then, the supported claim is only mechanistic plausibility, not demonstrated MC benefit.
