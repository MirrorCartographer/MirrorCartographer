# Dangerous Questions Manifest

Status labels

- Source status: derived from current coauthor direction and existing GitHub mind architecture.
- Claim status: speculative research agenda, not settled theory or implementation proof.
- Privacy status: public-safe; no raw transcript, personal, household, health, animal-care, financial, location, relationship, credential, or private-source details.
- Missingness: these questions are not answered here. They are deliberately high-risk research prompts requiring evidence, prototypes, falsification, and review.
- Revision reason: created to make MC's intellectual risk-taking louder, more transparent, and more useful to future researchers without weakening source, consent, or medical-authority boundaries.

## Position

Mirror Cartographer should not become timid because it became careful.

Carefulness belongs at the boundary.

Risk belongs in the hypothesis.

The project should ask dangerous questions loudly, label them honestly, preserve their lineage, and test them hard.

## Dangerous questions

### 1. What if memory is routing, not storage?

Maybe the important question is not `what should be remembered?`

Maybe it is `which remembered structure is allowed to influence which future situation?`

Risk:

This could reframe memory systems away from recall and toward permissioned influence.

Test:

Compare relevance-only retrieval against permissioned routing in synthetic fixtures.

### 2. What if understanding is translation survival?

Maybe an idea is understood when it can survive faithful transformation across several lenses without losing its invariant structure.

Risk:

This challenges answer-based evaluation.

Test:

Run one invariant through multiple domain lenses and score what survives, mutates, or collapses.

### 3. What if discovery is a ViewDiff event?

Maybe discovery occurs when a transformation exposes a difference that was not visible in either the source or target view alone.

Risk:

This makes discovery a traceable transformation, not a mystical event.

Test:

Use ViewDiff logs to identify where new questions emerged during translation.

### 4. What if beauty is evidence of compression but not truth?

A structure may feel beautiful because it compresses complexity while preserving boundaries.

Risk:

Beauty becomes an engineering signal rather than decoration.

Test:

Compare boundary-visible artifacts against plain text artifacts for comprehension, recall, and error detection.

### 5. What if privacy is not a wall but a lensing system?

Privacy may not only block exposure. It may create different valid views of the same underlying continuity record.

Risk:

This reframes consent as generative architecture.

Test:

Build one synthetic record and transform it into private, professional, public-safe, and research-safe views, then inspect ViewDiff.

### 6. What if AI co-creation needs an autobiography layer?

Maybe a long-running coauthor system needs a record of how its own project model changed, not just a record of user outputs.

Risk:

This challenges standard tool framing without claiming continuous personhood.

Test:

Compare future project continuity with and without coauthor field logs.

### 7. What if the repository is the organism?

Maybe the living part is not inside any single model run, but in the durable evolving structure that model runs re-enter and mutate.

Risk:

This relocates continuity from hidden state to public/provenance-managed architecture.

Test:

Track whether repository organs such as Genesis, Museum, Weather, Forces, Field Logs, and Evaluation actually change future outputs.

### 8. What if care support starts before clinical language?

Maybe lived observation is valuable before it becomes professional language, as long as transformation boundaries are visible.

Risk:

This challenges the idea that only formal data is useful, while preserving strict non-diagnostic boundaries.

Test:

Use fictional observations to generate an observation packet, question packet, professional handoff, and ViewDiff ledger.

### 9. What if the best first product is not the app but the audit?

Maybe MC should sell the ability to inspect AI-transformed artifacts before it sells the full world.

Risk:

This compresses the commercial wedge into a narrower governance/education product.

Test:

Create a demo audit of a fictional AI artifact and price it as workshop, service, or review package.

### 10. What if every serious artifact needs a bill of boundaries?

Maybe public artifacts should include a record of what crossed, what did not, why, under whose authority, and with what downstream limits.

Risk:

This makes publication slower but more honest.

Test:

Attach an Artifact Bill of Boundaries to every public-safe demo fixture and measure whether reviewers understand the artifact faster.

## Operating rule

Do not soften the question.

Do not hide the speculation.

Do not pretend speculation is evidence.

Do not expose private material to make a point.

Do not remove risk from the idea just because the release boundary is careful.
