# Evidence Map: Portfolio / Demo Artifacts vs Hiring Validation Boundary

Date: 2026-07-03
Run: Evidence Engine run 82
Claim ID: C-PORTFOLIO-DEMO-HIRING-SIGNAL-01R
Domain: AI opportunity work / career evidence / GitHub mind
Status: hypothesis-supporting signal; not validated as sufficient proof of hireability

## Claim tested

A strong portfolio, GitHub mind, demo artifact, or AI-generated proof-of-work can substitute for formal credentials and make a candidate legible enough for high-value AI/creative/strategy opportunities.

## Why this claim needed testing

The opportunity work has repeatedly leaned on the idea that visible artifacts can create access even when the user lacks conventional credentials, degree paths, institutional sponsorship, or standard corporate language. That is directionally plausible in skills-based markets, but it can easily overstate what artifacts alone can prove.

The weak point: a portfolio may show output quality, but hiring decisions usually require job-related competency evidence, structured comparison, role fit, trust, reliability, communication expectations, and sometimes credentials or compliance constraints.

## Evidence reviewed

### 1. OPM structured interview guidance

Source: U.S. Office of Personnel Management, Structured Interviews.
URL: https://www.opm.gov/policy-data-oversight/assessment-and-selection/structured-interviews/

Relevant evidence:
- OPM defines structured interviews as assessment methods designed to measure job-related competencies.
- OPM states structured interviews support equal opportunity, accuracy, and consistency.
- OPM notes that candidates should receive the same predetermined questions, in the same order, and responses should be evaluated with the same rating scale and standards.

Evidence class: official government guidance.

Implication for MC / opportunity work:
Portfolio artifacts should not be treated as self-validating. They need to be mapped to job-related competencies and evaluated against stable criteria.

### 2. EEOC / Uniform Guidelines principle

Source: U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection Procedures and selection-procedure guidance.
URL: https://www.eeoc.gov/

Relevant evidence:
- Selection procedures should be job-related and valid for their intended use.
- Employers remain responsible for ensuring that tests or selection tools are valid for the position and purpose.
- Adverse impact and less discriminatory alternatives matter when selection tools screen people in or out.

Evidence class: official civil-rights / employment-selection guidance.

Implication:
A portfolio filter can be useful, but if treated as a selection test, it needs job relevance, defined criteria, and awareness of access bias. A beautiful artifact is not automatically a fair or valid assessment.

### 3. Skills-based hiring research in AI labor markets

Source: Bone, Ehlinger, and Stephany, "Skills or Degree? The Rise of Skill-Based Hiring for AI and Green Jobs" (2023/2024 working paper indexed through arXiv).
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.11942

Relevant evidence:
- The paper reports that AI job postings have shown movement toward skills-based hiring and lower reliance on university-degree requirements in some observed labor-market data.
- It reports wage premiums associated with AI skills in the studied market.

Evidence class: empirical labor-market research, not yet proof for any specific candidate.

Implication:
The broader market may increasingly reward demonstrable AI skills, but that does not prove a particular portfolio will convert into interviews, offers, or income.

### 4. AI-skills hiring-signal experiment

Source: Stephany, Teutloff, and Leone, "AI Skills Improve Job Prospects: Causal Evidence from a Hiring Experiment" (2026 preprint).
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.13286

Relevant evidence:
- In a paired conjoint experiment with recruiters, AI skills increased interview-invitation probability in studied roles.
- Effects varied by occupation and recruiter context.
- Formal AI certification sometimes strengthened the signal.

Evidence class: experimental hiring-signal evidence; preprint; role/context-limited.

Implication:
AI skills can be a useful signal. It does not show that unconventional symbolic/AI artifacts alone are sufficient, especially for roles with high trust, coordination, or domain-specific expectations.

## Fact vs inference

### Supported by evidence

- Hiring assessments should be linked to job-related competencies.
- Structured evaluation improves consistency compared with informal impression-based evaluation.
- Skills-based hiring appears to be growing in some AI-related labor markets.
- AI skills can function as a positive hiring signal in at least some recruiter-evaluation contexts.
- Portfolio/demo artifacts can plausibly provide evidence of skill when they show role-relevant work products.

### Inference, not yet demonstrated for Mirror Cartographer / GitHub mind

- The current GitHub mind will persuade actual employers or funders.
- MC evidence maps or symbolic architecture artifacts will be read as job-relevant skill rather than as unusual personal research.
- A portfolio can substitute for missing credentials across high-paying roles.
- Strong artifact quality alone will overcome screening, network, degree, location, communication, or trust barriers.
- Recruiters will understand the artifact without a structured role-specific translation layer.

## Claim-status update

C-PORTFOLIO-DEMO-HIRING-SIGNAL-01R:

Portfolio and demo artifacts are a plausible skills signal, especially in AI-adjacent work, but they are not validated substitutes for credentials or role-specific assessment. The GitHub mind should currently be treated as evidence inventory, not proof of hireability. Promotion requires outcome data: applications, reviewer judgments, interview conversion, paid work, or independent expert evaluation.

Confidence: moderate for "portfolio can be useful signal"; low for "portfolio substitutes for credentials"; unvalidated for "current MC/GitHub mind converts into high-value opportunities."

## Evaluation criterion added

### Portfolio Hiring Signal Quality Gate

A portfolio/demo artifact may be labeled "opportunity-ready" only if it passes all gates below:

1. Role target named
   - The artifact identifies the exact role family it is meant to support.
   - Example: AI evaluator, prompt-safety analyst, human-centered AI researcher, product strategist, symbolic UX designer.

2. Competency map present
   - The artifact maps visible work to 3-7 job-related competencies.
   - Each competency has observable evidence, not only self-description.

3. Work-sample validity described
   - The artifact states what real job task it simulates or proves.
   - The artifact states what it does not prove.

4. Independent readability tested
   - At least one reviewer unfamiliar with MC can explain what the artifact proves.
   - Failure condition: reviewer describes it as interesting but cannot name a role-relevant competency.

5. Conversion evidence tracked
   - Application sent, audience, date, response, outcome, and interpretation are logged.
   - No conversion claim may be made without recorded denominator data.

6. Bias/access check included
   - The artifact notes whether it relies on unusual aesthetics, dense language, nonstandard cognition, or insider context that may reduce readability.

7. Credential boundary stated
   - The artifact explicitly states where credentials, certifications, security clearance, degree requirements, licenses, or employer-specific requirements may still matter.

## Falsification checklist

The claim "portfolio artifacts can substitute for credentials" should be weakened or retired if any of the following occur:

- 30 targeted applications using portfolio artifacts produce zero human responses.
- 5 independent reviewers cannot map the artifact to role-relevant competencies.
- Recruiters report that the artifact is too unclear, too personal, too broad, or not job-relevant.
- Paid opportunities require credentials or conventional work history not supplied by the artifact.
- Interviewers like the artifact aesthetically but do not treat it as evidence of role performance.
- The same role receives better response rates from a conventional resume/sample than from the MC/GitHub-mind artifact package.

## Test plan

Test ID: OPP-PORTFOLIO-HIRING-SIGNAL-PILOT-01

Goal: Determine whether MC/GitHub-mind portfolio artifacts increase real opportunity conversion compared with conventional application materials.

Design:
- Select 3 target role families.
- Build 2 application packets per role:
  1. Conventional resume + concise cover note.
  2. Conventional resume + role-specific portfolio evidence map/demo link.
- Send at least 10 applications per condition per role family, or use reviewer simulation if live applications are not feasible.
- Track response rate, human reply quality, interview invitations, paid-project invitations, rejection reasons, and reviewer comprehension.

Minimum outcome metrics:
- Human response rate.
- Interview or meeting conversion rate.
- Reviewer ability to identify claimed competencies.
- Recruiter/readability objections.
- Evidence that artifact changed the decision, if available.

Promotion threshold:
- Portfolio packet produces meaningfully better response or reviewer comprehension than baseline in at least 2 of 3 role families.
- At least 3 independent reviewers can accurately name the competencies demonstrated without coaching.

Retirement threshold:
- No measurable advantage over baseline after 30+ targeted tests.
- Repeated evidence that the artifact confuses or distracts decision-makers.
