# Force — Custody Survives Interruption

## Force statement

**Interruption stops action; it does not dissolve responsibility.**

A workflow can pause, a model can be blocked, a vendor can fail, a clinician can become unavailable, a caregiver can burn out, a record can be corrected, and an organization can deactivate an agent. None of those events automatically answer who is responsible for preservation, communication, escalation, repair, or safe return.

## Pressure

Modern agentic systems create chains of delegated work. When those chains break, risk concentrates at the break point:

- unsupported claims may remain in circulation;
- partially completed actions may be mistaken for completed actions;
- records may become stale but still trusted;
- access credentials may remain active after the use case ends;
- users may not know who can safely receive context;
- human oversight may exist on paper but not at the moment custody transfers.

## Repository implication

The Governance Kernel must eventually support a failover state:

- normal custody;
- interrupted custody;
- fallback custody;
- escalated custody;
- returned custody;
- retired custody.

Each state should have an authority ceiling and evidence requirement.

## Mirror Cartographer translation

For Mirror Cartographer, this matters because MC often handles material that can feel personally important, health-adjacent, emotionally charged, symbolically dense, or operationally useful.

The public-safe boundary remains:

- MC may organize observations;
- MC may preserve uncertainty;
- MC may support communication;
- MC may show missingness;
- MC may suggest grounded next steps;
- MC must not impersonate clinical, legal, financial, or emergency authority.

Failover custody makes that boundary stronger by naming what happens when the normal path cannot continue.
