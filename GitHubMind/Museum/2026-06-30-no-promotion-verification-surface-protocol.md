# Museum — No Promotion: Verification Surface Protocol

## Status

Public-safe anti-overclaim artifact.

## Artifact held back

Verification Surface Protocol.

## Why this belongs in the Museum

The protocol is useful, but it can be over-promoted.

A visible verification surface does not automatically make an artifact true, safe, compliant, clinically valid, legally defensible, or complete. It only makes boundaries easier to inspect.

## Do not claim

Do not claim that the Verification Surface Protocol:

- proves factual truth;
- creates legal compliance;
- creates medical safety;
- prevents hallucination;
- guarantees provenance survival;
- prevents metadata stripping;
- replaces expert review;
- certifies an AI system;
- solves social-care documentation risk;
- makes derivative artifacts automatically trustworthy.

## What can be claimed

The protocol can claim only this:

A derivative or exported artifact is safer to rely on when its source, claim, privacy, missingness, revision, authority, and correction boundaries remain inspectable at the point of reuse.

## Failure risks

- Surface theater: a claim card exists but the underlying evidence is weak.
- Label laundering: low-quality claims appear legitimate because they have polished provenance labels.
- Metadata fragility: content credentials or metadata can be stripped by platforms or transformations.
- False comfort: users assume visible labels equal validation.
- Care overreach: a care-support verification card is mistaken for clinical authority.

## Required boundary language

Use:

"This verification surface exposes provenance and uncertainty boundaries. It does not independently validate the claim."

## Practical lane caution: income

An AI Verification Surface Review should be sold as a governance documentation and risk-reduction service, not as certification, insurance, legal compliance, or safety guarantee.

## Practical lane caution: care and social-care

A Care Summary Verification Card should support review, continuity, and correction. It must not diagnose, treat, triage, or override qualified care judgment.

## Source labels

- Runtime governance research supports stronger evidence and audit surfaces.
- Content provenance work shows that metadata and watermarking can help but are not foolproof.
- Social-care and medical AI reporting shows that documentation surfaces can influence real decisions and liability.

## Claim labels

- Claim: the Verification Surface Protocol is useful only if its limits are visible.
- Confidence: high.
- Evidence maturity: design-stage synthesis.
- Authority: Museum boundary, not legal, clinical, compliance, or certification authority.

## Privacy label

Public-safe.

No implementation test, customer validation, legal review, clinical review, or automated repository scan has been completed.

## Revision label

Revise if implementation evidence shows verification surfaces reduce specific error rates or improve review behavior.
