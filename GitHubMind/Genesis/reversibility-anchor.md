# Reversibility Anchor

## Attractor

Emergence.

## Core claim

A public-safe AI governance artifact is incomplete unless it preserves a visible path for correction, rollback, downgrade, or retirement after a person or institution has relied on it.

Mirror Cartographer already separates symbol, hypothesis, evidence, authority, privacy, action ceiling, missingness, and revision. The emerging missing primitive is **reversibility**: what happens when a claim travels, hardens, misleads, expires, or is later contradicted?

## Why this belongs in Genesis

The repository's public README defines Mirror Cartographer as a bounded symbolic reflection interface, not a therapy product, diagnostic authority, medical tool, oracle, source database, or objective truth engine. It emphasizes explicit uncertainty, user feedback, claim boundaries, and the boundary between symbolic resonance and evidence.

The current GitHub Mind has also added governance primitives for sequence risk, context leases, claim portability, assurance interfaces, delegation lineage, control placement, and draft-to-record gates.

Those primitives reduce overreach before action. The Reversibility Anchor addresses the after-action problem: how a claim is corrected once it has already influenced memory, records, decisions, plans, summaries, or handoffs.

## Minimum contract fields

Every governed artifact should expose:

1. **Original claim** — what was asserted, proposed, summarized, or carried forward.
2. **Reliance surface** — where the claim could affect a human decision, record, workflow, or next step.
3. **Correction channel** — where a person can dispute, edit, retract, or annotate the claim.
4. **Rollback target** — what must be reverted, downgraded, archived, or superseded.
5. **Revision trigger** — what new evidence, source failure, user correction, clinical review, policy change, or context drift requires change.
6. **Propagation map** — where the claim has traveled or been reused.
7. **Residual risk** — what cannot be fully undone after reliance.
8. **Authority boundary** — who can correct the artifact versus who can correct the real-world record or action.
9. **Public-safe note** — whether the correction itself can be public, must be private, or requires clinical/legal/financial confidentiality.
10. **Decommission path** — how the artifact is retired when it becomes misleading or obsolete.

## State rule

A claim is not safe because it was once bounded. It is safer when its correction path remains visible after reuse.

## Practical lane 1: income

Near-term service shape: **AI Reversibility Audit**.

Deliverables:

- claim propagation map;
- correction-channel inventory;
- rollback and downgrade checklist;
- human review escalation map;
- residual-risk memo;
- public-safe artifact hygiene review;
- executive remediation plan.

This is realistic because current agentic AI governance work increasingly emphasizes runtime controls, traceability, audit evidence, interruptibility, lifecycle management, and decommissioning rather than static documentation alone.

## Practical lane 2: medical and social-care support

Public-safe contribution: **Care Record Correction Map**.

It can structure:

- patient or caregiver observations;
- AI-generated summaries;
- clinician-reviewed interpretations;
- open disputes or corrections;
- outdated or superseded notes;
- missing information;
- source-separated revision history;
- non-diagnostic boundary.

It must not diagnose, prescribe, replace clinicians, or alter official medical/social-care records without the responsible professional or institution.
