## Research findings used

### 1. Interoceptive machine framework

A 2026 review proposes interoception-inspired AI architectures using homeostatic, allostatic, and enactive principles for adaptive autonomy and uncertainty-sensitive regulation. This does not mean MC should pretend to feel. It means MC can borrow a design abstraction: internal-state labels, uncertainty re-evaluation, and interaction-based updating.

MC translation: a reflection should ask, "what state is being organized here?" not only "what does this symbol mean?"

### 2. Human agency and interface causality

A 2026 HCI paper argues that high-stakes AI problems are not only about trust, but preserving human causal control through interfaces that represent uncertainty and system state.

MC translation: the interface must show what was inferred, remembered, corrected, and bounded. Hidden interpretation is bad UI.

### 3. Mental-health AI safety evaluation disagreement

A 2026 mental-health AI safety paper found poor agreement among expert evaluators on safety-critical AI responses, implying that high-stakes reflective AI should preserve disagreement and uncertainty rather than collapse it into one confident answer.

MC translation: MC should support multiple interpretations, claim labels, correction memory, and non-coercive grounding.

### 4. Contextual memory intelligence

Recent reflective AI memory work frames memory as adaptive infrastructure for longitudinal coherence, explainability, drift detection, and rationale preservation.

MC translation: memory is not a box of facts about the user. It is a living audit trail of context, uncertainty, revision, and user-approved meaning.

### 5. AI safety and agent governance

Recent AI safety and agent-governance discourse emphasizes guardrails, oversight, evaluation, and the risk of opacity as systems become more agentic.

MC translation: MC's public advantage is not intensity. Its advantage is inspectability.

## Product wedge: Uncertainty Weather

A more fun, visual interface direction:

- Symbols are not verdicts; they are weather patterns.
- Memory is not a courtroom; it is a trail map with marked fog.
- Corrections are not failures; they are changed wind direction.
- Evidence is not vibe; it is terrain.
- The user is not decoded; the user is the navigator.

UI elements:

1. **Fog band** — unclear or low-evidence material.
2. **Pressure zone** — repeated emotional/somatic pressure.
3. **Trail marker** — user-approved symbolic association.
4. **Lightning mark** — high-intensity but not-yet-grounded inference.
5. **Anchor stone** — verified fact or user-confirmed boundary.
6. **Wind shift** — correction or revision.
7. **Safe path** — grounded next step.

## Product wedge: State Shift Card

A single MC output card should contain:

- Current state: user-stated sensation, symbol, or scene.
- Pattern echo: only if user-approved or clearly repeated.
- Possible pressure: hypothesis, not fact.
- Evidence boundary: what supports it and what does not.
- Uncertainty weather: clear, foggy, stormy, split, or grounded.
- Correction slot: what the user can reject or refine.
- Grounding move: one small concrete next action.
- Next observation: what to notice later.

## Synthesis

The fun version of MC is not less rigorous. It is a visible weather system for meaning-making.

The machine should not say, "I know what you are."

It should say, "Here is the map I am drawing, here is the fog, here is the trail I inferred, here is where you corrected me, here is what is only symbolic, here is what has evidence, and here is the next safe step."

That is the capability: beautiful uncertainty with a spine.
